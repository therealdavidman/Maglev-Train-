## Required materials:

- A bench power supply
- A low-carbon dx8mm lx35mm steel rod
- 28 AWG wire
- 22 AWG pvc coated lead wires
- a multimeter
- kitchen scale
- a non metalic holder for the magnet
- A clicker
- soldering iron
- neodymium magnets
- a thermoucoupler
  

##construction
- using the 28 AWG wire, begin tightly wrapping it around the steel rod leaving ~5 mm gap on each side of the rod.
- wrap tightly in a back and forth pattern making sure to not leave any gaps
- after the first 100 turns add a wire tap. then after every consecutive 50 turns add another tap.
- continue coiling until reaching 600 turns
- secure the thermocoupler to the middle of the electromagnet using kapton tape
- secure a neodymium magnet to the scale and the electromagnet to the non metalic holder


##procedure:
  1. measure the coils resistance at each tap and record. (does it match our theoritical resistance?)
  2. begin by attatching the ground to the negative of the power supply and the positive to the first tap.
  3. place the electromagnet against the neodymium magnet.
  4. measure the initial temperature
  5. turn the power supply on and set the voltage to 0.
  6. set the current limit to 0.10 amps
  7. connect the coil
  8. slowly raise voltage until the the supply reaches target current
  9. record the voltage, current, and initial temperature.
  10. reading the measured weight on the scale, convert using F = mg and record the force
  11. record the temperature at 30s intervals until reaching thermal equilibrium
  12. raise the electromagnet to 2mm and record
  13. raise to 4 then 6, then 8
  14. repeat steps 3 - > 11 for 0.20A, 0.30A, 0.5A, 1A (being sure to wait for the coil to return to room temperature before restarting)
  15. be cautious at 1A the wattage may climb too high for the coil and should be measured frequently 
  16. repeat steps 2 - > 12 for each tap
